Interview Questions & Answers

Click any question to expand the ideal, average, and red-flag answers. Use these to quickly evaluate where a candidate lands during the real interview.

1

Can you walk me through the nail services you're most confident performing?

Skills & Experience
▾
💎 Ideal Answer
"I'm most confident with full sets of acrylics, hard gel, and builder gel overlays. I also do gel manicures with nail art — I've been practicing ombre, chrome powder, and 3D designs for the past year. I can do pedicures including callus removal, and I'm comfortable with dip powder as well. I brought some photos if you'd like to see."
🌸 Average Answer
"I'm good at regular gel manicures and basic acrylics. I'm still learning nail art but I'm working on it. I've done pedicures too, mostly in school and during my apprenticeship."
⚠️ Red Flag Answer
"I can do everything — nails, acrylics, whatever you need." (No specifics, no portfolio, vague confidence without substance.)
2

How do you keep your station and tools sanitized between clients?

Health & Safety
▾
💎 Ideal Answer
"After every client I remove all single-use items, wipe down my station with an EPA-registered disinfectant, and put metal tools through a proper wet disinfection process — I use barbicide or similar for the required contact time. Any files or buffers that can't be disinfected properly get tossed. I also wash my hands between every client and wear gloves when needed."
🌸 Average Answer
"I wipe everything down with disinfectant spray and use new files for each client. I know sanitation is really important so I try to be careful."
⚠️ Red Flag Answer
"I clean everything between clients — I've never had anyone get an infection so I must be doing it right." (No process, no awareness of protocols, using results as a substitute for standards.)
3

Tell me about a time a client wasn't happy with their nails. What happened and how did you handle it?

Client Relations & Problem Solving
▾
💎 Ideal Answer
"A client came back saying her gel had lifted on two nails after three days. I apologized, looked at the nails, and did a complimentary fix right away. Afterwards I asked her some questions and realized she'd been using hand sanitizer heavily — I hadn't mentioned that as a caution beforehand. I've made sure to include aftercare instructions in every appointment since then."
🌸 Average Answer
"I've had a client who didn't like the colour after she got home. I told her to come back in and we changed it. She was happy after that."
⚠️ Red Flag Answer
"Honestly most of my clients have been happy. If someone complains it's usually because they didn't take care of their nails properly." (Deflects blame to client — major concern for customer retention.)
4

How do you stay current with nail trends and new techniques?

Growth & Learning
▾
💎 Ideal Answer
"I follow a lot of nail artists on Instagram and TikTok — I pay attention to what's trending seasonally. I also took an advanced nail art course last year and I'm looking at a gel sculpting course for this spring. I try to practice new techniques on myself or friends before offering them to clients."
🌸 Average Answer
"I follow some nail accounts online and try to keep up with what clients are asking for. I haven't done any extra courses yet but I'd be open to it."
⚠️ Red Flag Answer
"I've been doing nails for a while so I pretty much know what works. Trends come and go." (Closed to growth — will fall behind in a trend-driven industry.)
5

This is a small woman-owned business in North Bay. How do you feel about working in a tight-knit team environment?

Culture Fit & Attitude
▾
💎 Ideal Answer
"That's actually exactly what I'm looking for. I've worked in a bigger salon before and honestly I prefer a smaller environment where there's real communication and you're not just a number. I think the client relationships are more meaningful too — people become regulars, not just appointments."
🌸 Average Answer
"I think that sounds nice. I get along well with people and I don't mind working closely with others."
⚠️ Red Flag Answer
"As long as everyone does their own thing and stays out of my space I'm fine." (Low team compatibility — likely to create friction in a small studio environment.)
6

What would you do if you noticed a client had signs of a nail fungus or skin infection when they came in for their appointment?

Professional Judgment
▾
💎 Ideal Answer
"I would politely and privately let the client know what I'm seeing and explain that I'm not able to perform the service on an affected area — it's for their safety and other clients' too. I'd recommend they see a doctor and let them know they're welcome to rebook once it's cleared. I'd document it in case there's any follow-up needed."
🌸 Average Answer
"I would tell them I can't do their nails until it's looked at by a doctor. I'd try to be nice about it so they don't feel embarrassed."
⚠️ Red Flag Answer
"I'd probably just avoid that area and do the rest of the nails." (This is both a health code violation and a liability — immediate disqualifier.)
7

Do you have references from previous salons or clients we can contact?

Verification & Trust
▾
💎 Ideal Answer
"Yes, absolutely. I have two former salon managers and a few long-term clients who said they'd be happy to speak on my behalf. I also have Google reviews from clients I've served if that helps. Here's my contact sheet."
🌸 Average Answer
"I have one reference from my last salon — I can get you the number. I don't have clients who've formally agreed but I could ask a couple."
⚠️ Red Flag Answer
"I don't really keep in touch with my old bosses. We didn't end on the best terms." (No references + conflict history = significant risk.)

Green Flags & Red Flags

A quick-reference guide for Amanda to use during and after interviews. Trust your instincts — but use this list to back them up.

✅ Green Flags — Signs of a Great Hire

Brings a Portfolio Unprompted

They didn't wait to be asked — they came ready to show their work. That level of preparation says everything.

Asks About Your Clients

A candidate who asks "what's your typical client like?" is already thinking about fit, not just a paycheque.

Specific About Sanitation

They name products, processes, and contact times without being prompted. This protects you legally and your clients physically.

Speaks Positively About Past Workplaces

Even if they left on hard terms, a professional candidate talks about what they learned — not what went wrong.

Actively Learning Outside Work

Courses, tutorials, social media practice — they invest their own time into getting better. That's rare and valuable.

Comfortable with Client Redos

They've had an unhappy client and handled it gracefully. No ego, just resolution. That's a service professional.

🚩 Red Flags — Walk Away

Cannot Provide Certification

In Ontario, practising without a valid licence is illegal and puts your business at serious risk. No exceptions.

Blames Clients for Complaints

If they can't take accountability in an interview, they won't take it in front of your clients either.

Vague or Evasive About Past Jobs

"It just wasn't a good fit" for every single past employer is a pattern, not bad luck.

Dismissive of Sanitation Questions

One infection complaint can damage your reputation permanently in a small city like North Bay. Don't risk it.

First Questions Are About Pay & Days Off

Compensation matters — but leading with it before understanding the role signals the wrong priorities.

No Portfolio & No Explanation

Any experienced nail tech has photos of their work somewhere. No portfolio with no reason is a significant gap.

💡 Amanda's Hiring Tip

Consider doing a short paid practical trial of 2–3 hours before making a final offer. Ask the candidate to perform a gel manicure on a willing model (a friend, family member, or yourself). You'll see their technique, their communication with the client, their station hygiene, and their confidence under light observation — all things an interview alone can't reveal.
